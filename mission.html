About the researcher

Hi, I'm Anthony.

I'm a student researcher at Milton Academy with a background in robotics, mechanical engineering, and human-centered design. Regenix began as a science fair project and grew into something I genuinely believe could change how rehabilitation works for millions of people.

I've spent the past year studying biomechanics, building prototypes, running experiments, and learning that the hardest engineering problems aren't the ones on the spec sheet — they're the ones that only appear when you actually try to help a real person.

My goal isn't to build the most powerful exoskeleton. It's to build the one that actually reaches the people who need it.

School Milton Academy Year 2025 Location Milton, MA
Mission statement To make powered knee rehabilitation accessible to the 38 million people in the United States — and the billions more globally — who are currently priced out of the technology that could restore their mobility.
The problem behind the problem
Why I built this, specifically.

When I first looked into powered exoskeletons, I expected to find a spectrum of options — affordable ones and expensive ones. What I found instead was a cliff edge: devices at $7,000 and above, then nothing. The gap between a $30 compression sleeve and a $7,000 powered device is not a market opportunity — it's a structural failure that leaves the patients with the greatest need with the worst options.

Regenix is designed around a single constraint: it has to cost less than a typical insurance co-pay. The $200 bill of materials isn't a happy accident. It's the load-bearing assumption the entire design is built around. Every component, every material choice, every architecture decision flows from that number.

The semi-rigid chain, the cable-driven actuation, the on-device AI that avoids expensive cloud infrastructure — all of it exists because the price point is the mission. Getting the biomechanics right matters enormously. But a perfect device that costs $50,000 helps no one new.
